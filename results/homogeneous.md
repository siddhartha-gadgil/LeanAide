# Homogeneous pseudo-length functions on groups

This note is written as a source document for formalization.  All groups are written
multiplicatively unless an abelian group is explicitly written additively.

## Basic definitions

**Definition 1 (Pseudo-length function).**
Let $G$ be a group with identity element $e$. A function
$l : G \to \mathbb{R}$ is a *pseudo-length function* on $G$ if the following
four conditions hold.

1. **Nonnegativity:** for every $g \in G$, $0 \leq l(g)$.
2. **Identity:** $l(e) = 0$.
3. **Symmetry:** for every $g \in G$, $l(g^{-1}) = l(g)$.
4. **Triangle inequality:** for every $g,h \in G$, $l(gh) \leq l(g) + l(h)$.

The nonnegativity condition is included explicitly so that the codomain can be
the usual real numbers in a formalization.  Equivalently, one may take the
codomain to be the set of nonnegative real numbers and omit the separate
nonnegativity axiom.

**Definition 2 (Length function).**
Let $G$ be a group with identity element $e$. A pseudo-length function
$l : G \to \mathbb{R}$ is a *length function* if, for every $g \in G$,
$g \neq e$ implies $0 < l(g)$.

**Definition 3 (Homogeneous pseudo-length function).**
Let $G$ be a group. A pseudo-length function $l : G \to \mathbb{R}$ is
*homogeneous* if, for every $g \in G$ and every integer $n \in \mathbb{Z}$,
one has
$$
l(g^n) = |n|\,l(g).
$$

In particular, for every $g \in G$, homogeneity gives $l(g^2)=2l(g)$.

**Definition 4 (Commutator).**
Let $G$ be a group. For elements $x,y \in G$, define the commutator
$$
[x,y] := xyx^{-1}y^{-1}.
$$

**Definition 5 (Commutator subgroup).**
Let $G$ be a group. The *commutator subgroup* $[G,G]$ is the subgroup of $G$
generated by the set $\{[x,y] \mid x,y \in G\}$.

**Definition 6 (Abelianization).**
Let $G$ be a group. The *abelianization* of $G$ is the quotient group
$$
G_{\mathrm{ab}} := G/[G,G].
$$
Let $\pi_{\mathrm{ab}} : G \to G_{\mathrm{ab}}$ denote the quotient
homomorphism.

**Definition 7 (Torsion subgroup of an abelian group).**
Let $A$ be an abelian group, written additively. The torsion subgroup $T(A)$ is
the subgroup
$$
T(A) := \{a \in A \mid \exists n \in \mathbb{Z},\ n \neq 0 \text{ and } n a = 0\}.
$$

**Definition 8 (Torsion-free abelian group).**
An abelian group $A$ is *torsion-free* if $T(A)=\{0\}$.

## General lemmas about homogeneous pseudo-lengths

**Lemma 1 (Conjugation invariance).**
Let $G$ be a group. Let $l : G \to \mathbb{R}$ be a homogeneous
pseudo-length function. Then for every $x,y \in G$,
$$
l(yxy^{-1}) = l(x).
$$

**Proof.**
Fix $x,y \in G$.  We first prove $l(yxy^{-1}) \leq l(x)$.

Let $n$ be a positive integer.  By homogeneity,
$$
n\,l(yxy^{-1}) = l((yxy^{-1})^n).
$$
The group identity $(yxy^{-1})^n = yx^n y^{-1}$ gives
$$
n\,l(yxy^{-1}) = l(yx^n y^{-1}).
$$
By the triangle inequality,
$$
l(yx^n y^{-1}) \leq l(y) + l(x^n) + l(y^{-1}).
$$
By symmetry, $l(y^{-1})=l(y)$.  By homogeneity, $l(x^n)=n\,l(x)$.  Hence
$$
n\,l(yxy^{-1}) \leq 2l(y)+n\,l(x).
$$
Dividing by the positive real number $n$ gives
$$
l(yxy^{-1}) \leq l(x)+\frac{2l(y)}{n}.
$$
Since this inequality holds for every positive integer $n$, and
$2l(y)/n$ tends to $0$, it follows that
$$
l(yxy^{-1}) \leq l(x).
$$

For the reverse inequality, define $z := yxy^{-1}$.  Then
$x=y^{-1}zy$.  Applying the inequality already proved with $z$ in place of
$x$ and $y^{-1}$ in place of $y$ gives
$$
l(x)=l(y^{-1}zy) \leq l(z)=l(yxy^{-1}).
$$
The two inequalities imply $l(yxy^{-1})=l(x)$.

**Lemma 2 (Conjugation-invariant triangle iteration).**
Let $G$ be a group. Let $l : G \to \mathbb{R}$ be a homogeneous
pseudo-length function. Let $w,y,z,s,t \in G$.  For every natural number
$n$, define
$$
C_n := (wy)^n s^{-1}t(zw^{-1})^n.
$$
Then
$$
l(C_n) \leq l(s^{-1})+l(t)+n(l(y)+l(z)).
$$

**Proof.**
We prove the claim by induction on $n$.

For $n=0$, the definition gives $C_0=s^{-1}t$.  By the triangle inequality,
$$
l(C_0)=l(s^{-1}t) \leq l(s^{-1})+l(t).
$$
This is the desired inequality for $n=0$.

Assume the inequality holds for a natural number $n$.  The definition gives
$$
C_{n+1}=wy C_n z w^{-1}.
$$
Equivalently,
$$
C_{n+1}=w(y C_n z)w^{-1}.
$$
By Lemma 1, applied to the element $yC_nz$ and the conjugating element $w$,
$$
l(C_{n+1})=l(yC_nz).
$$
By two applications of the triangle inequality,
$$
l(yC_nz) \leq l(y)+l(C_n)+l(z).
$$
By the induction hypothesis,
$$
l(C_n) \leq l(s^{-1})+l(t)+n(l(y)+l(z)).
$$
Combining the last two inequalities gives
$$
l(C_{n+1})
\leq l(s^{-1})+l(t)+(n+1)(l(y)+l(z)).
$$
This proves the induction step.

Therefore the inequality holds for every natural number $n$.

**Lemma 3 (Splitting lemma).**
Let $G$ be a group. Let $l : G \to \mathbb{R}$ be a homogeneous
pseudo-length function. Let $a,w,y,z,s,t \in G$. Assume
$$
a=s(wy)s^{-1}
$$
and
$$
a=t(zw^{-1})t^{-1}.
$$
Then
$$
l(a) \leq \frac{l(y)+l(z)}{2}.
$$

**Proof.**
Let $n$ be a positive integer.  From the two hypotheses and the standard
identity for powers of conjugates, we have
$$
a^n=s(wy)^n s^{-1}
$$
and
$$
a^n=t(zw^{-1})^n t^{-1}.
$$
Hence
$$
a^{2n}=a^n a^n
      =s(wy)^n s^{-1}t(zw^{-1})^n t^{-1}.
$$
By homogeneity,
$$
2n\,l(a)=l(a^{2n}).
$$
Using the displayed expression for $a^{2n}$ and the triangle inequality,
$$
l(a^{2n})
\leq l(s)+l((wy)^n s^{-1}t(zw^{-1})^n)+l(t^{-1}).
$$
By Lemma 2,
$$
l((wy)^n s^{-1}t(zw^{-1})^n)
\leq l(s^{-1})+l(t)+n(l(y)+l(z)).
$$
By symmetry, $l(s^{-1})=l(s)$ and $l(t^{-1})=l(t)$.  Therefore
$$
2n\,l(a) \leq 2l(s)+2l(t)+n(l(y)+l(z)).
$$
Dividing by $2n$ gives
$$
l(a) \leq \frac{l(y)+l(z)}{2}+\frac{l(s)+l(t)}{n}.
$$
Since this inequality holds for every positive integer $n$, and
$(l(s)+l(t))/n$ tends to $0$, we obtain
$$
l(a) \leq \frac{l(y)+l(z)}{2}.
$$

## The commutator estimate

The next lemma is the combinatorial input used to prove that homogeneous
pseudo-length functions vanish on commutators.  It is stated as a separate
lemma because its proof is a finite calculation in the group laws followed by
Lemma 3.

**Lemma 4 (Commutator recurrence).**
Let $G$ be a group. Let $l : G \to \mathbb{R}$ be a homogeneous
pseudo-length function. Fix $x,y \in G$ and set $c:=[x,y]$.  Define
$f : \mathbb{Z}\times\mathbb{Z}\to\mathbb{R}$ by
$$
f(m,k):=l(x^m c^k).
$$
Then for every $m,k \in \mathbb{Z}$,
$$
f(m,k)\leq \frac{f(m-1,k)+f(m+1,k-1)}{2}.
$$

**Proof.**
Fix $m,k \in \mathbb{Z}$.  Define
$$
a:=x^m c^k,
$$
$$
w:=x,
$$
$$
u:=x^{m-1}c^k,
$$
and
$$
v:=y^{-1}x^m c^{k-1}xy.
$$
Then
$$
wu=x(x^{m-1}c^k)=x^m c^k=a.
$$
Thus $a$ is conjugate to $wu$, because $a=wu$.

We next prove that $a$ is conjugate to $vw^{-1}$.  Since $w=x$,
$$
vw^{-1}=y^{-1}x^m c^{k-1}xyx^{-1}.
$$
Using the commutator identity
$$
c=xyx^{-1}y^{-1},
$$
we have
$$
xyx^{-1}=cy.
$$
Therefore
$$
vw^{-1}
=y^{-1}x^m c^{k-1}cy
=y^{-1}x^m c^k y
=y^{-1}ay.
$$
Hence $a=y(vw^{-1})y^{-1}$, so $a$ is conjugate to $vw^{-1}$.

Lemma 3, applied to the elements $a,w,u,v$, gives
$$
l(a)\leq \frac{l(u)+l(v)}{2}.
$$
By the definition of $u$,
$$
l(u)=l(x^{m-1}c^k)=f(m-1,k).
$$
It remains to identify $l(v)$.  The element $v$ is conjugate to
$x^{m+1}c^{k-1}$ because
$$
yvy^{-1}=x^m c^{k-1}x
$$
and
$$
x(x^m c^{k-1}x)x^{-1}=x^{m+1}c^{k-1}.
$$
By Lemma 1, conjugate elements have the same length.  Therefore
$$
l(v)=l(x^{m+1}c^{k-1})=f(m+1,k-1).
$$
Substituting the formulas for $l(u)$ and $l(v)$ into the inequality from
Lemma 3 gives
$$
f(m,k)\leq \frac{f(m-1,k)+f(m+1,k-1)}{2}.
$$

**Lemma 5 (Random-walk iteration of the recurrence).**
Let $G$ be a group. Let $l : G \to \mathbb{R}$ be a homogeneous
pseudo-length function. Fix $x,y \in G$ and set $c:=[x,y]$.  Define
$f(m,k):=l(x^m c^k)$.  Let $n$ be a natural number.  Let
$\epsilon_1,\ldots,\epsilon_{2n}$ be independent random variables with
$\mathbb{P}(\epsilon_i=1)=\mathbb{P}(\epsilon_i=-1)=1/2$.  Define
$$
S_{2n}:=\epsilon_1+\cdots+\epsilon_{2n}.
$$
Since $2n$ is even, $S_{2n}$ is an even integer. Hence $-S_{2n}/2$ is an
integer-valued random variable. Then
$$
f(0,n)\leq \mathbb{E}\bigl[f(S_{2n},-S_{2n}/2)\bigr].
$$

**Proof.**
Lemma 4 says that, for every $m,k \in \mathbb{Z}$,
$$
f(m,k)\leq
\frac{1}{2}f(m-1,k)+\frac{1}{2}f(m+1,k-1).
$$
Let $\epsilon$ be a Rademacher random variable. The preceding inequality is
equivalent to
$$
f(m,k)\leq
\mathbb{E}\bigl[f(m+\epsilon,k-(1+\epsilon)/2)\bigr].
$$
Indeed, the case $\epsilon=-1$ gives $(m-1,k)$, and the case $\epsilon=1$
gives $(m+1,k-1)$.

Starting at $(0,n)$ and applying the preceding inequality successively with
$\epsilon_1,\ldots,\epsilon_{2n}$ gives
$$
f(0,n)\leq
\mathbb{E}\bigl[f(M_{2n},K_{2n})\bigr],
$$
where
$$
M_{2n}:=\epsilon_1+\cdots+\epsilon_{2n}
$$
and
$$
K_{2n}:=n-\sum_{i=1}^{2n}\frac{1+\epsilon_i}{2}.
$$
The first coordinate is $M_{2n}=S_{2n}$. Also,
$$
\sum_{i=1}^{2n}\frac{1+\epsilon_i}{2}
=\frac{2n+S_{2n}}{2}
=n+\frac{S_{2n}}{2}.
$$
Therefore $K_{2n}=-S_{2n}/2$. Substituting these formulas for $M_{2n}$ and
$K_{2n}$ gives
$$
f(0,n)\leq \mathbb{E}\bigl[f(S_{2n},-S_{2n}/2)\bigr].
$$

**Lemma 6 (Square-root bound for the random walk).**
Let $n$ be a natural number.  Let
$S_{2n}:=\epsilon_1+\cdots+\epsilon_{2n}$, where
$\epsilon_1,\ldots,\epsilon_{2n}$ are independent Rademacher random variables.
Then
$$
\mathbb{E}(|S_{2n}|)\leq \sqrt{2n}.
$$

**Proof.**
By Jensen's inequality applied to the concave function $u\mapsto \sqrt{u}$ on
$[0,\infty)$,
$$
\mathbb{E}(|S_{2n}|)
=\mathbb{E}\bigl(\sqrt{S_{2n}^2}\bigr)
\leq \sqrt{\mathbb{E}(S_{2n}^2)}.
$$
The variables $\epsilon_i$ have mean $0$ and variance $1$.  Since the
$\epsilon_i$ are independent,
$$
\mathbb{E}(S_{2n}^2)=2n.
$$
Therefore $\mathbb{E}(|S_{2n}|)\leq\sqrt{2n}$.

**Proposition 7 (Homogeneous pseudo-lengths vanish on commutators).**
Let $G$ be a group. Let $l : G \to \mathbb{R}$ be a homogeneous
pseudo-length function. Then for every $x,y \in G$,
$$
l([x,y])=0.
$$

**Proof.**
Fix $x,y \in G$ and define $c:=[x,y]$.  Let $n$ be a positive integer.
By homogeneity,
$$
n\,l(c)=l(c^n)=f(0,n),
$$
where $f(m,k):=l(x^m c^k)$.

By Lemma 5,
$$
f(0,n)\leq \mathbb{E}\bigl[f(S_{2n},-S_{2n}/2)\bigr].
$$
For every even integer $r$,
$$
f(r,-r/2)=l(x^r c^{-r/2}).
$$
By the triangle inequality,
$$
l(x^r c^{-r/2})\leq l(x^r)+l(c^{-r/2}).
$$
By homogeneity,
$$
l(x^r)=|r|\,l(x)
$$
and
$$
l(c^{-r/2})=|r|\,l(c)/2.
$$
Therefore
$$
\mathbb{E}\bigl[f(S_{2n},-S_{2n}/2)\bigr]
\leq \mathbb{E}(|S_{2n}|)\,(l(x)+l(c)/2).
$$
By Lemma 6,
$$
\mathbb{E}(|S_{2n}|)\leq\sqrt{2n}.
$$
Combining these inequalities gives
$$
n\,l(c)\leq \sqrt{2n}\,(l(x)+l(c)/2).
$$
Dividing by the positive real number $n$ gives
$$
l(c)\leq \frac{\sqrt{2n}}{n}\,(l(x)+l(c)/2).
$$
The right-hand side tends to $0$ as $n$ tends to infinity.  Hence
$l(c)\leq 0$.  By nonnegativity of the pseudo-length function, $0\leq l(c)$.
Thus $l(c)=0$.

Since $x$ and $y$ were arbitrary, $l([x,y])=0$ for all $x,y \in G$.

## Factorization through abelianization

**Lemma 8 (Vanishing on the commutator subgroup).**
Let $G$ be a group. Let $l : G \to \mathbb{R}$ be a homogeneous
pseudo-length function. Then for every $g \in [G,G]$,
$$
l(g)=0.
$$

**Proof.**
By Proposition 7, $l([x,y])=0$ for every commutator $[x,y]$.
By symmetry, the inverse of any commutator also has length $0$.
Every element of $[G,G]$ is a finite product of commutators and inverses of
commutators.  Let
$$
g=q_1q_2\cdots q_r
$$
be such a product, where each $q_i$ is a commutator or the inverse of a
commutator.  Then $l(q_i)=0$ for every $i$.  Repeated use of the triangle
inequality gives
$$
l(g)\leq l(q_1)+\cdots+l(q_r)=0.
$$
By nonnegativity, $0\leq l(g)$.  Therefore $l(g)=0$.

**Lemma 9 (Factorization through the abelianization).**
Let $G$ be a group. Let $l : G \to \mathbb{R}$ be a homogeneous
pseudo-length function.  Then there exists a unique function
$$
\bar l : G_{\mathrm{ab}}\to \mathbb{R}
$$
such that
$$
l=\bar l\circ \pi_{\mathrm{ab}}.
$$
Moreover, $\bar l$ is a homogeneous pseudo-length function on the abelian group
$G_{\mathrm{ab}}$.

**Proof.**
Define $\bar l(\pi_{\mathrm{ab}}(g)):=l(g)$ for $g\in G$.  We first prove
that this definition is independent of the chosen representative.

Assume $\pi_{\mathrm{ab}}(g)=\pi_{\mathrm{ab}}(h)$.  Then $h^{-1}g\in [G,G]$.
By Lemma 8, $l(h^{-1}g)=0$.  Since $g=h(h^{-1}g)$, the triangle inequality
gives
$$
l(g)\leq l(h)+l(h^{-1}g)=l(h).
$$
Similarly, $g^{-1}h\in [G,G]$, and the triangle inequality applied to
$h=g(g^{-1}h)$ gives $l(h)\leq l(g)$.  Hence $l(g)=l(h)$.
Thus $\bar l$ is well-defined.

The identity $l=\bar l\circ\pi_{\mathrm{ab}}$ holds by definition of
$\bar l$.  Since $\pi_{\mathrm{ab}}$ is surjective, this identity also proves
uniqueness of $\bar l$.

The pseudo-length axioms for $\bar l$ follow from the corresponding axioms for
$l$ and from the fact that $\pi_{\mathrm{ab}}$ is a group homomorphism.
For example, if $a=\pi_{\mathrm{ab}}(g)$ and $b=\pi_{\mathrm{ab}}(h)$, then
$$
\bar l(ab)=\bar l(\pi_{\mathrm{ab}}(gh))=l(gh)
\leq l(g)+l(h)=\bar l(a)+\bar l(b).
$$
The proofs of nonnegativity, identity, symmetry, and homogeneity are analogous.

**Lemma 10 (Vanishing on torsion).**
Let $A$ be an abelian group, written additively. Let
$p : A\to\mathbb{R}$ be a homogeneous pseudo-length function, where
homogeneity is written as $p(na)=|n|p(a)$ for
$n\in\mathbb{Z}$ and $a\in A$.  If $a\in T(A)$, then $p(a)=0$.

**Proof.**
Assume $a\in T(A)$.  By definition of $T(A)$, there exists
$n\in\mathbb{Z}$ such that $n\neq 0$ and $na=0$.  By homogeneity,
$$
p(na)=|n|p(a).
$$
Since $na=0$ and $p(0)=0$, the left-hand side is $0$.  Thus
$$
|n|p(a)=0.
$$
Since $n\neq 0$, $|n|>0$.  Therefore $p(a)=0$.

**Lemma 11 (Factorization through the torsion-free quotient).**
Let $A$ be an abelian group. Let $p:A\to\mathbb{R}$ be a homogeneous
pseudo-length function. Then $p$ factors through the quotient
$$
A/T(A).
$$
The induced function on $A/T(A)$ is again a homogeneous pseudo-length function.

**Proof.**
By Lemma 10, $p$ vanishes on $T(A)$.  The same representative-independence
argument used in Lemma 9 shows that $p$ descends to a well-defined function on
$A/T(A)$.  The quotient map is a homomorphism, so the pseudo-length axioms and
homogeneity descend to the quotient.

## Extension to a normed vector space

**Lemma 12 (Extension from a torsion-free abelian group to a rational vector space).**
Let $A$ be a torsion-free abelian group. Let $p:A\to\mathbb{R}$ be a
homogeneous pseudo-length function.  Let
$$
V_{\mathbb Q}:=A\otimes_{\mathbb Z}\mathbb Q.
$$
There exists a seminorm $\|\cdot\|_{\mathbb Q}$ on $V_{\mathbb Q}$ such that
$$
\|a\otimes 1\|_{\mathbb Q}=p(a)
$$
for every $a\in A$.

**Proof.**
Since $A$ is torsion-free, the canonical map
$A\to A\otimes_{\mathbb Z}\mathbb Q$ is injective.  We identify $A$ with its
image in $V_{\mathbb Q}$.

Every element of $V_{\mathbb Q}$ can be written as $a/n$ with
$a\in A$ and $n$ a positive integer.  Define
$$
\|a/n\|_{\mathbb Q}:=\frac{p(a)}{n}.
$$
We prove that this definition is independent of the chosen representation.
Assume $a/n=b/m$ in $V_{\mathbb Q}$, with $m,n$ positive.  Then
$ma=nb$ in $A$.  By homogeneity,
$$
m\,p(a)=p(ma)=p(nb)=n\,p(b).
$$
Dividing by $mn$ gives $p(a)/n=p(b)/m$.  Hence the definition is
well-defined.

Nonnegativity follows from nonnegativity of $p$.  The value of the seminorm at
$0$ is $0$ because $p(0)=0$.  If $q\in\mathbb Q$ and $v\in V_{\mathbb Q}$,
then the identity $\|qv\|_{\mathbb Q}=|q|\|v\|_{\mathbb Q}$ follows by writing
$q=r/s$ with $r\in\mathbb Z$ and $s$ positive, and then applying homogeneity
of $p$.

For the triangle inequality, write $u=a/n$ and $v=b/m$, with $m,n$ positive.
Then
$$
u+v=(ma+nb)/(mn).
$$
By the triangle inequality for $p$ on the abelian group $A$,
$$
p(ma+nb)\leq p(ma)+p(nb).
$$
By homogeneity, $p(ma)=m\,p(a)$ and $p(nb)=n\,p(b)$.  Therefore
$$
\|u+v\|_{\mathbb Q}
\leq \frac{m\,p(a)+n\,p(b)}{mn}
=\frac{p(a)}{n}+\frac{p(b)}{m}
=\|u\|_{\mathbb Q}+\|v\|_{\mathbb Q}.
$$
Thus $\|\cdot\|_{\mathbb Q}$ is a seminorm on $V_{\mathbb Q}$.

**Lemma 13 (Passage to a real Banach space).**
Let $V_{\mathbb Q}$ be a rational vector space equipped with a seminorm
$\|\cdot\|_{\mathbb Q}$.  There exist a real Banach space $B$ and a
$\mathbb Q$-linear map
$$
\iota:V_{\mathbb Q}\to B
$$
such that
$$
\|\iota(v)\|_B=\|v\|_{\mathbb Q}
$$
for every $v\in V_{\mathbb Q}$, after quotienting by the zero-seminorm
subspace if necessary.

**Proof.**
Let
$$
N:=\{v\in V_{\mathbb Q}\mid \|v\|_{\mathbb Q}=0\}.
$$
The seminorm axioms imply that $N$ is a rational vector subspace of
$V_{\mathbb Q}$.  The quotient $V_{\mathbb Q}/N$ carries a norm induced by
$\|\cdot\|_{\mathbb Q}$.  Extend scalars from $\mathbb Q$ to $\mathbb R$ and
equip the resulting real vector space with the induced norm.  Let $B$ be the
metric completion of this normed real vector space.  The completion is a real
Banach space by definition.  The canonical composite
$$
V_{\mathbb Q}\to V_{\mathbb Q}/N\to B
$$
has the stated norm-preserving property.

## Main theorem

**Theorem 14 (Classification of homogeneous pseudo-length functions).**
Let $G$ be a group. Let $l : G\to\mathbb{R}$ be a homogeneous pseudo-length
function.  Then there exist a real Banach space $B$ and a group homomorphism
$$
\phi:G\to (B,+)
$$
such that
$$
l(g)=\|\phi(g)\|_B
$$
for every $g\in G$.

Moreover, if $l$ is a length function, then $\phi$ can be chosen to be
injective.

**Proof.**
By Lemma 9, $l$ factors through a homogeneous pseudo-length function
$$
\bar l:G_{\mathrm{ab}}\to\mathbb{R}.
$$
By Lemma 11, $\bar l$ factors through the torsion-free quotient
$$
A:=G_{\mathrm{ab}}/T(G_{\mathrm{ab}}).
$$
Let
$$
\tilde l:A\to\mathbb{R}
$$
be the induced homogeneous pseudo-length function.

By Lemma 12, $\tilde l$ extends to a seminorm on
$$
V_{\mathbb Q}:=A\otimes_{\mathbb Z}\mathbb Q.
$$
By Lemma 13, there exist a real Banach space $B$ and a canonical map
$V_{\mathbb Q}\to B$ preserving the seminorm.

Define $\phi:G\to B$ to be the composite
$$
G
\xrightarrow{\pi_{\mathrm{ab}}}
G_{\mathrm{ab}}
\to
A
\to
V_{\mathbb Q}
\to
B.
$$
Each arrow in this composite is a group homomorphism when $B$ is regarded as
an additive group.  Hence $\phi$ is a group homomorphism.  By construction,
for every $g\in G$,
$$
l(g)=\|\phi(g)\|_B.
$$

Assume now that $l$ is a length function.  We prove that $\phi$ is injective.
Let $g\in G$ and assume $\phi(g)=0$.  Then
$$
l(g)=\|\phi(g)\|_B=\|0\|_B=0.
$$
Since $l$ is a length function, $l(g)=0$ implies $g=e$.  Therefore the kernel
of $\phi$ is trivial, and $\phi$ is injective.

## Corollary

**Corollary 15.**
Let $G$ be a group.  The group $G$ admits a homogeneous length function if and
only if $G$ is abelian and torsion-free.

**Proof.**
First assume that $G$ admits a homogeneous length function $l$.
By Theorem 14, there exist a real Banach space $B$ and an injective group
homomorphism
$$
\phi:G\to (B,+).
$$
The additive group $(B,+)$ is abelian.  Every subgroup of an abelian group is
abelian.  Since $\phi$ is injective, $G$ is isomorphic to the subgroup
$\phi(G)$ of $(B,+)$.  Hence $G$ is abelian.

We next prove that $G$ is torsion-free.  Let $g\in G$, and assume there exists
a positive integer $n$ such that $g^n=e$.  Homogeneity gives
$$
n\,l(g)=l(g^n).
$$
Since $g^n=e$, the right-hand side is $l(e)=0$.  Thus $n\,l(g)=0$.
Since $n>0$, $l(g)=0$.  Since $l$ is a length function, $g=e$.
Therefore every torsion element of $G$ is the identity element, so $G$ is
torsion-free.

Conversely, assume that $G$ is abelian and torsion-free.  Write the group law
additively.  Since $G$ is torsion-free, the canonical map
$$
G\to G\otimes_{\mathbb Z}\mathbb R
$$
is injective.  Let
$$
V:=G\otimes_{\mathbb Z}\mathbb R.
$$
Choose a Hamel basis of the real vector space $V$.  Define a norm on $V$ by
transporting any standard norm from the free real vector space on that basis;
for example, one may use the $\ell^1$ norm on the finite coordinate support
of each vector.  Denote this norm by $\|\cdot\|$.

Define
$$
l(g):=\|g\otimes 1\|
$$
for $g\in G$.  Since $G\to V$ is injective and $\|\cdot\|$ is a norm,
$l(g)>0$ whenever $g\neq 0$.  Also,
$$
l(0)=\|0\|=0,
$$
$$
l(-g)=\|-g\|=\|g\|=l(g),
$$
and
$$
l(g+h)=\|g+h\|\leq \|g\|+\|h\|=l(g)+l(h).
$$
For every integer $n$,
$$
l(ng)=\|n(g\otimes 1)\|=|n|\,\|g\otimes 1\|=|n|\,l(g).
$$
Thus $l$ is a homogeneous length function on $G$.

This proves both directions.
